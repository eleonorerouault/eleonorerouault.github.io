* ***Cognition Long Time after Weather Shocks in China*** (JMP)
<div style="text-align: justify"> Abstract: This study examines the long-term effects of exposure to weather shocks from in utero to age 15 on cognition levels and decline assessed among rural Chinese individuals aged 50 to 80. Utilizing cognitive, agricultural, and meteorological data spanning 1931 to 2020, the methodology identifies the channels through which these shocks affect cognitive development. While extensive literature has documented the immediate effects of environmental factors on early-life health and human capital, this paper expands the analysis to long-term outcomes into adulthood and old age. The findings highlight the critical influence of the middle childhood period on long-term cognitive outcomes. Experiencing a one standard deviation increase in weather shocks from birth to age 4 results in a decrease in cognitive scores by 0.05 standard deviations, an effect comparable to the cognitive decline seen over 1.5 years of aging. I show that prenatal weather shocks lead to an accelerated latent decline in cognitive functions among males, observable after age 65. Finally, weather-induced increases in crop yields between ages 5 and 15 reduce cognitive scores among females due to a trade off in investment in human capital in favor of boys and detrimental to girls. </div>
